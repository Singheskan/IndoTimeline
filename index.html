Timeline of my Semester in Indonesia

Arrival

The flight was long, really long, 24 hours from Frankfurt to Jakarta with an eight hour stop in Doha. I had a lot of time checking out the airport which was easily explorable through a futuristic, mostly glass-made railway driving above the heads of mere citizens which were eager to find their gates by foot. After arriving in Jakarta and passing the border control I was greeted by students of the Universitas Indonesia (UI) dressed in bright, yellow suit jackets (looking as bad as it sounds). Hasbi was one of them which accompanied me and William, another student from Darmstadt, to our apartments at the Margonda Residence in Depok. As we were on the road around 5pm we were not the only ones trying to get their way to Jakarta. The traffic was unbelievable and scooter drivers insane in their confidence to maneuver through this see of vehicles. It was intense but we made it out alive as it seems to be a general practice of Indonesians to drive in such circumstances. My buddy Desti welcomed me at the residence and showed me my apartment in the allegedly 16th floor of the building. (The elevator misses out floor 14 and 15 as they’re not selectable) Afterwards, I could catch a good night of sleep, even though an Islamic priest woke me up at 4 in the morning. It was a good day.

12.08.19

Traffic

The Imam woke me up again. I used my free sleepless time to find out how to embed videos. The traffic is really insane!

13.08.19

Orientation

It is crazy how living in a completely different country can become simple routine in just a week. The crowded shattering of a docent vehicles fades to white noise while I manage to find my way through Depok (city on the southern border of Jakarta) by myself. My co-students and I underwent several events in the last days, including orientation days to get to know the campus and the library (which is apparently the biggest in south-east Asia), as well as team building events to get to know each other. Surprisingly, everyone seems to get along independent of cultural, religious, or national differences. The Indonesian people are so proactive and helpful that anxieties of any kind are never present. Another student was capturing this feeling very good by saying that: “I am so cherished and cared for that I do not feel as I have left home.” One of the events was the Orientation Day in which we were introduced into some organizational topics as well as cultural aspects of Indonesia, including a local dance performed bare foot with a plate in each hand. Those plates were circularly moved while dancing and sometimes even moved on without breaking. I have never seen something that exotic before. In the last days I had also enough time to organize my apartment, buy a second hand guitar in Jakarta and spend a whole day in Jakarta’s tourist districts. As it seems, we tourists are part of the attractions as well, especially the Caucasian looking exchange students, as several inhabitants asked them frequently to take pictures with them. Even I was asked twice! Today I had the chance to meet Dr. Ade Azurat a member of the faculty of computer science at UI who gave me a tour through the campus and even introduced me to everyone I might contact in the future, including the head of Master students, secretariat, and faculty speaker. He is a very nice guy with a great taste of humor. I am planning to take a Bioinformatics and Advanced Computer Vision class. As I am working as a freelancer for Fraunhofer I will probably not find the time to take more courses. (Besides seeing the country of course)

19.08.19

Culture & Nature

The area around Jakarta is filled with various mysterious places and stores in which you can easily loose yourself. One of them was a miniature park representing different areas of Indonesia, including Bali, Sumatra, and Borneo. The UI Buddy Club organized a tour for us in which we were introduced to the cultural differences between the different parts of Indonesia. Those differences were shown in examples of ceremonial dresses, tools & weaponry, as well as construction and art. Even though the regions uniformed as one country they are still remarkably unique and differentiable from each other in every way, even in language. Though there exists an official language, Bahasa Indonesia (literally translated to “Language Indonesia”), every region speaks their own language as well. Bahasa is more of a stripped down to the basic language used for communication with the Dutch heirs in the early days. Beside of cultural insights I had finally some time to spend in nature! Bogor, a city located under Depok, hosts a beautiful botanical garden as well as a resident of the Indonesian president. It is filled with various in Latin-labeled plants and trees of which my roommate could have made more sense of than me (as he focusses on Plant Biology). Nevertheless, I could still admire the beauty of it to the fullest.

26.08.19

1000 Islands

Pulau Seribu (“1000 Islands”) is a chain of actually around 110 islands located north-west of Jakarta. We were staying for three days at Pramuka Island and exploring the surrounding islands from there including the National Marine Park, a sanctuary for Hawksbill Sea Turtles and local sharks. You can find the location of our stay in the attached Google Map. (I can only use the Google API cost-free in developer mode, sorry for the inconvenience) The locals’ life a lay-back life fishing, dancing, and singing all day and do not worry too much about the hectic life of their fellow citizens in Jakarta. We had a chance to calm down and were indulging paradise for a little bit. I saw some kids fighting like in a series I watched as a kid called “Naruto”. Somehow that made me cringe but also nostalgic at the same time.

As it turns out, Bioinformatics is not available this semester which is why I switched to Advanced Machine Learning instead. The master students have a dedicated room for studying, likewise the bachelor and degree students. Luckily, I was able to make some new friends. I was really glad as I am the only exchange student in computer science this semester. I was just sitting down for a few hours and already had a few helping hands for my upcoming Bahasa Indonesia class or any other obstacles as well as a few new numbers to call. 15.09.19

Riot

The Indonesian people have voted and the government has spoken - by revising the criminal code and planing to legislate absolutely hilarious laws that I must post in this blog. My top three: Publicly criticising the president (3 years jail), Rape victims go to prison if they abort the baby (4 years jail) and the best one, if you have a chicken in your house and the chicken trespasses onto your neighbours' lawn, you'll be jailed as well - absolutely ridiculous. Of course, I have double checked the resources and as it turns out it was from some kind of twitter account of which I did not find any further origins. Nevertheless, my buddies (like every local Indonesian student I have met) say these proposed laws are really serious, which would explain why the riots in Jakarta last weekend were escalating completely. Allegedly, two students from another university even died. Of course, I cannot say these are facts but rather hearsay. We exchange students were repeatedly asked, even begged not to go to Jakarta at all for the whole week, even officially by the International Office of UI. One should also mention, that we foreigners do not have the right to protest here and will be deported immediately if arrested even near protest grounds. What a wonderful, free country I am living in. I do not really know how I should think about this. Two days after the riots I went to the national orchestra in Jakarta, as I did not want to miss it a second time. (The first time was because I sliced my foot up on a coral). Though I would really like to go to the protests, a friend of mine made not a good but a fair point by saying that the "Indonesian people must fight their own battles, and know their own situation the best". Although, I will stay undercover (which is anyway necessary as I have way to much to do right now), this does not really satisfy me. - Let me conclude with the following lines from Les Miserables - Do you hear the people sing? Singing the songs of angry men? It is the music of the people Who will not be slaves again! When the beating of your heart Echoes the beating of the drums There is a life about to start When tomorrow comes!

28.09.19

Escape from Depok

We were visiting Yogyakarta over the weekend to see the cultural area of Indonesia and catch a fresh breather, like literally, the air was amazing. We took a 8h train ride across East to West Java and spend two days in Yogya (as the locals call it) visiting several temples, including Borobudur and Prambanan, two giant buddhist and hinduistic temples and checking out the amazing food. We were staying at the Los Manos Hostel which I can highly recommend to anyone who goes there. The check-in guy we met was also kind enough to drive us around the city, as the taxi company did not have enough drivers at the moment. If you are there, ask for Andy! On the last night before my departure I met a guy from Poland and chatted a little with him. His family lives near the hometown of my grandparents. After I was casually small-talking about my computer science studies he was asking me, if I know the Honey Badger project. Allegedly, he was working for a company collaborating with Facebook about 10 years ago as a, let us say, "human filter". He had to filter out all inappropriate content which was not recognized by the filters at that time and told us some really horrible stuff, though he gladly did not go into any details. Crazy dude, crazy stories, still I do not know if I can believe him, it is just too much of a coincidence.

07.10.19

Wilderness

My Machine Learning professor is somehow very good at communicating different content to different people. Two weeks prior I was working 24/7 on several assignments, including a paper review about Adversarial Examples against Deep Neural Networks. It was a very interesting topic as it somehow strapped the power of the big Machine Learning hype we have experienced in the last years. Especially, the One-Pixel Attack against Convolutional Neural Networks was very surprising as it managed to falsify image classification by only altering one pixel. I finished my review before the 1st October as I thought we had to do. I also asked some other classmates just to be sure but only got more confused. As it turned out, we only had to do a research proposal and had time to finish the review until December. Of course, my professor was quite surprised when I told him that I am already finished. Well, less work for me in the coming days. Because of that I could use the last two weekends to visit some places in Java, like Yogyakarta or Ujung Kulon last weekend. The sub-island Ujung Kulon is maybe one of the most untouched peaces of nature I have ever seen. I could not find any piece of trash at all and the beaches looked like directly taken out of an advertisement. Wild animals, like makak monkeys, pigs, and deers were just walking around the beaches. The cheeky monkeys were always on a stroll to steal our stuff, mostly without success but always eager to try again. The national park is also a harbour for the last remaining 60 Javanese Rhinos. Except of a sunburn and not enough sleep it was a wonderful experience.

14.10.19

Mt. Salak

Nico Kettler (Kiedla), my good friend from Germany visited me over the last week. Truth be told, it was a big relief for mee to see someone from my old circle again. When you spend so much time abroad away from family and friends you grow custom to the character shifts and changes you subconsciously make. Although, you know deep inside that the people you have met in the last months can not, and will not understand you as good as the ones you have known for years. I have introduced him to my daily life in Depok, showed him my university, some buddies, and a lot of traditional food. Gladly, his stomach was prepared for this culinary expedition. We spend a whole day in Jakarta visiting the harbour, fish market, old town, chinatown, Belanda Bridge, and city central. We met an old man at the harbour which gave us a insightful tour to all places we wanted to visit, also enabling us to enter some mahogany transport ships which were setting for Sumatra. It was an amazing opportunity. Besides, he organized a ride in a classical vehicle, a three-wheeled car called Pajai, in which he guided us through Jakarta to our destinations. We spend the night getting totally lost in the rush hour but escaped after four hours (keep in mind, that Depok is only about 40km away from central Jakarta). Besides visiting the Botanical Garden in Bogor, I finally had the chance to go hiking on Mt. Salak. Kiedla, a buddy from my university, and I spend two days and one night on the mysterious volcano. It was engulfed in a mystical fog which rendered it impossible to see how long the way was. Due to heavy rain before our arrival at the foot of the mountain the way was very difficult to pass. Slippery mud made it impossible to walk in a fast pace. Nevertheless, we made it safely to the camping place. Although we wanted to reach the mountain top as well, we decided not to, as it would have been way to dangerous. Coming sunrise we made our way to the viewpoint which was easier accessible from the camping place. Also, the way was easier to walk on on the second day. The vista point was unbelievable. Sulfur smog emerged out of several craters and ascended into the sunny sky. The smell was disgusting but the view tremendous.

07.11.19

Lombok

Right after Kiedla went back home another friend from high school visited me, Noah Siegler. He had only one goal, which was learning to surf, so I asked a few locals which places they could recommend us. Obviously, Bali was a very prominent place, though I did not want to go somewhere too touristic which is why we went to Lombok, the neighboring island of Bali. The first thing we realised was that a ridiculous amount of Germans were staying there during our visit. We met several people from Munich, including our hotel hostess, as well as one girl which even lives near our hometowns, coming from Mannheim (let us call her Eve). It was easy to find someone who can teach us to surf, we called him "Chef" as we could not remember his name. Also, he was always behaving like a real Chef, always lecturing us about how to read the waves and other typical surfer-guy-stuff. Speaking of stereotypes, I must say that all of the surfer guys we talked to met the description. Though they were hilarious, they were mostly running around after girls and rarely lost themselves in too complicated topics which made conversations with them rarely interesting but often hilarious. We did not only socialize with a lot of local surfers on the beaches but had also the unbelievable opportunity to see a real fisher village and a traditional wedding, but let me get back a few steps for this story. When we met Eve, we also met two guys from Austria, who were telling us this crazy story about a bar called "Small but Spicy" near the main crossroad in Kuta. They asked us to meet them there one evening which we of course did. Arriving there, it turned out that the bar consisted of a few plastic chairs, a surfboard which was transfunctionalized to a table and a small bar waggon. We could not believe it but still decided to stay there for a while. After half a hour, when our faith was at the lowest, the others finally arrived - half a hour later the whole place was cramped! That was when we met O'Reilly, on of the big guys, also called "Legend" by the locals, who is responsible for managing the real-estate in this area of Kuta. After shortly talking to us he suddenly rushed of to some guy coming by on a scooter, came back, and asked us swiftly if we wanted to attend the wedding of his cousin tomorrow. After some healthy contemplation and suspicion we accepted. The next day he guided us to a place in which we got traditional clothing for the wedding, including a Sarung, a fancy stitched cloth comparable to a colorful kilt, a dagger, and a bandana. Afterwards, we spend noon exploring his village, and headed out to the place of the parade. In traditional Balinese, and Lombokian fashion, the bride and groom marched in a big parade to the village of the bride's parents. We lost ourselves easily in middle of hundreds of dancing and singing locals. It was a moment you cannot buy for money, pure luck, the right place at the right time.
